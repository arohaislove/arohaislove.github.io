Introduction

Welcome to your SAR Boat Book audio guide. Today we're covering Chapter One: Crew Welfare. This is essential knowledge for anyone operating on a Coastguard rescue vessel, so let's get into it.

Section 1: The Big Picture

First up, the core principle. Coastguard has a responsibility to ensure the safety and wellbeing of all crew. The goal is simple: everyone goes home healthy and safe. That's not negotiable.

Section 2: IMSAFE - Your Personal Checklist

Before every departure, you need to run through what's called IMSAFE. It's a self-assessment system, and you need to confirm to the Master that you've done it and you're fit for duty. This gets recorded in the ship's log.

IMSAFE is an acronym. Let's break it down letter by letter.

I is for Illness. Ask yourself: Am I free of illness? Free of symptoms? Am I medically fit to be out there?

M is for Medication. Am I taking any medication, and is it safe? Will it affect my ability to drive or use machinery? And importantly - is there any medication I should have with me?

S is for Stress. Am I managing stress well? Both at home and at work. Stress affects judgment, and judgment keeps people alive.

A is for Alcohol. Am I free of alcohol and drugs, and free of their effects? This means not just "have I had a drink today" but "am I still affected from last night?"

F is for Fatigue Management. Am I rested? Have I been sleeping well? We'll talk more about fatigue in a moment because it's a big one.

E is for Eating. Am I fed and watered? Ready to go? You can't perform if you're running on empty.

So that's IMSAFE. Illness, Medication, Stress, Alcohol, Fatigue, Eating. Run through it before every job.

Section 3: Fatigue - The Critical Risk

Now let's dig into fatigue, because Coastguard considers this a critical risk. Not just a risk - a critical one. It must be carefully monitored during all activities on board.

Fatigue needs to be discussed during the pre-departure briefing. Not after you're already out there struggling to stay alert - before you leave. And it needs to be actively monitored while underway. The Master is ultimately responsible for crew safety and wellbeing, but everyone needs to be honest about how they're tracking.

Section 4: Fatigue Management Factors

When managing fatigue, you need to ensure there's sufficient crew on board. But what does "sufficient" mean? It depends on several factors. Let's run through them.

First: Nature of the tasking. What kind of job is it? A straightforward tow is different from a complex search pattern in rough conditions.

Second: Duration. How long is this going to take? A quick job close to base is very different from an extended operation.

Third: Location from base. How far are you going? The further out you are, the longer the return journey when everyone's already tired.

Fourth: Expected weather conditions. Rough weather is exhausting. It demands constant physical effort and mental focus.

Fifth: Crew's existing state of fatigue. What state is everyone in right now, before you even leave? Has someone just finished a night shift at their day job?

Sixth: Crew state of health. Is anyone carrying a niggling injury or feeling under the weather?

And seventh: Crew training and experience. Less experienced crew may fatigue faster because everything requires more conscious effort.

Conclusion

That's Chapter One: Crew Welfare. The key takeaways? Run IMSAFE before every departure. Take fatigue seriously - it's a critical risk. And remember, the goal is always the same: everyone goes home healthy and safe.
